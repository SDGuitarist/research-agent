## GOAL 1: Seasonal Planning Timeline

### When Do Hotels Set Next Year's Budgets?

The hotel industry's formal **budget season runs August through December**, with most properties beginning the process in August and finalizing in Q4.

**Key milestones:**

| Period | Activity | Source |
|--------|----------|--------|
| **August** | Performance data analysis begins; departments start projections | Lighthouse (hotel budget guide) |
| **September** | F&B Directors create entertainment budgets; cross-departmental collaboration | MHW Live Music (entertainment agency, 30+ markets) |
| **October–November** | Department-level budgets submitted; senior management review | Lighthouse; Mews; HFTP |
| **December** | Final budgets approved; implementation plans communicated | Industry standard |

**Source:** "Hotel budget season is a three-month period, often beginning in August, where revenue managers analyze past performance and strategically plan for the next year." — Lighthouse Revenue Management Guide (Tier 1)

**Source:** "Every September, I meet with F&B Directors who are scrambling to create their 2024 Entertainment Budget." — MHW Live Music (entertainment management company, offices in Miami, Austin, New Orleans, Scottsdale; Tier 2)

### Entertainment-Specific Budget Data

**Budget ranges at properties with regular entertainment programming:**
- **$100,000–$500,000+** annually for entertainment
- **3–20+ regularly scheduled local entertainment bookings weekly**
- Additional bookings for holidays and special activations

**Source:** MHW Live Music hotel services page (Tier 2)

**Entertainment as share of operating costs:**
- Entertainment falls under F&B operating costs or is treated as a separate operated department
- Typically **5–8% of operating expenses**, depending on the hotel's entertainment policy
- Budget model should include three layers: **baseline** (core schedule), **volume** (scales with occupancy), **event** (holidays, special shows)

**Source:** Finoko USALI-aligned hotel entertainment cost guide (Tier 1)

## GOAL 2: Properties That Went Dark and Restarted

### Documented Dark-to-Restart Cases

#### Case 1: Tonga Room & Hurricane Bar, Fairmont San Francisco

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Property** | Fairmont San Francisco, San Francisco, CA |
| **Dark period** | March 2020 – July 9, 2021 (~16 months) |
| **Trigger for closure** | COVID-19 pandemic |
| **Trigger for restart** | Market reopening; venue is signature draw for the hotel |
| **What returned** | Same format: Island Groove band on floating lagoon barge, $20 cover charge, Wed–Sat 7pm |
| **Format change** | Minimal — same band, same concept, same venue. Tiki/Polynesian theme preserved from 1945 origins |
| **Oscar connection** | Oscar Gonzalez worked as Executive Chef at Fairmont San Francisco 2016–2023. He was in the building during both the closure and the reopening. He experienced a dark-to-restart cycle firsthand. |
| **Source** | Mercury News (June 2021), Fairmont SF website, OpenTable (Tier 1/Tier 2) |

**Key insight:** The Tonga Room's live band has performed since 1945 — 80+ years. A 16-month closure during a global pandemic did not end the program. The format was restored without changes. This precedent: **even when entertainment is interrupted, legacy programming returns.**

### Why Hotels Cut Entertainment (and What Happens)

#### COVID-Era Cuts

Hotels cut entertainment along with other "non-essential" amenities during the pandemic:
- Daily housekeeping eliminated
- Restaurants and lounges closed or reduced
- Room service discontinued
- Pools and spas closed
- Live entertainment stopped immediately (social distancing made it impossible)

**Source:** The Points Guy, "2 Years Into COVID-19: If It's Still Gone From Hotels, Is It Ever Coming Back?" (Tier 2)

#### Guest Satisfaction Impact of Service Cuts

| Data Point | Value | Source |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| Hotel guest satisfaction decline | **Dropped 5% since 2018**, declined 3 quarters in a row | ACSI via Hotel Management (Tier 1) |
| Consumers who will spend less with cost-cutting brands | **87%** | UJET survey (1,600 consumers) (Tier 1) |
| Consumers who share bad experiences | **48%** | UJET survey (Tier 1) |
| Consumers who switch to competitors | **33%** | UJET survey (Tier 1) |

**Key quotes:**

> "Guests are going to be furious and are not going to accept that."
> — **Henry Harteveldt**, travel analyst (Tier 1)

> "You're paying more for less, and that's terrible value for your money."
> — **Tim Jue**, San Francisco travel reporter (Tier 2)

> At four-star hotels, guests "are paying for and expect service and amenities" — setting hotel owners up for "massive problems."
> — The Future of Commerce analysis (Tier 1)

#### Staffing Crisis as Barrier to Restart

- **97%** of hotels experienced staffing shortages post-COVID
- **49%** severely impacted
- Hotels will employ **1.97M by end-2022** (84% of pre-pandemic workforce)
- Entertainment hiring competes with core operational staffing needs

**Source:** American Hotel & Lodging Association via Future of Commerce (Tier 1)

#### The Recovery Trajectory

The live music industry has not just recovered — it has **surged past pre-pandemic levels:**

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| 2023-2024 live market vs. 2019 | **30–50% larger** | Statista (Tier 1) |
| Top 100 tours 2023 gross | **>$9B** (50% over 2022 record) | Statista (Tier 1) |
| UK hospitality venues increasing live music | **87%** | UK Live Music Index (Tier 1) |
| UK economic contribution of live music 2022 | **>$8B** | Grand View Research (Tier 1) |
| Average revenue increase per venue (UK) | **£107K/year** | UK Live Music Index (Tier 1) |

**Key insight:** The industry has overcorrected from COVID cuts. Hotels that cut entertainment are now in the **minority** — 87% of UK hospitality venues are increasing live music. The Lodge's dark status is increasingly anomalous as the industry accelerates entertainment investment.

## PFE Executive Summary

### Question 1: Is February–March the Right Time to Pitch for Summer Programming?

**Yes — but barely. The window is closing.**

The formal hotel budget cycle runs August–December. The Lodge's 2026 budget was set with zero entertainment allocation. This means Oscar can't add entertainment through the normal budget process — he must make an **operational decision** to reallocate within his F&B budget.

February–March is the operational planning window when F&B Directors are:
- Planning summer menus and seasonal activations
- Scheduling outdoor dining, patio service, pool programming
- Locking in vendor relationships for peak season
- Making decisions about guest experience enhancements

Evidence that the window is closing:
- Grand Del Mar has published summer 2026 entertainment dates through September
- MHW says F&B Directors "scramble" to create budgets in September — meaning entertainment is usually an annual commitment, not ad hoc
- Summer festivals lock lineups 6-9 months in advance (August–November for a June start)
- The Lodge's concierge said entertainment won't return until "probably summertime" — confirming Oscar is thinking about it but hasn't committed

**PFE timing implication:** Reaching Oscar in February–March is not early — it's **just in time.** By April–May, summer programming decisions will be locked or the season will be too close to implement properly. The pitch should frame urgency without pressure: "Summer programming decisions are being made now across your competitive set."

### Question 2: Is There a Documented Pattern of New Leadership Restarting Entertainment?

**Yes — across every documented case, new or returning leadership made entertainment a priority.**

| Case | Leadership Trigger | Outcome |
|------|-------------------|---------|
| Waldorf Astoria NYC | New F&B Director (Nicholas Bathurst) | Entertainment made central to 2025 reopening |
| Langham Hospitality Group | CEO strategic initiative (Bob van den Oord) | Created first-ever Group Director of Music |
| San Ysidro Ranch | Creative rebrand | Zero → nightly jazz |
| Tonga Room, Fairmont SF | Market reopening post-COVID | Signature entertainment restored immediately |
| Rosewood Hotel Georgia | Lobby transformation/rebrand | Branded "1927 Lobby Live" series created |
| Four Seasons Biltmore SB | Reopening after 6-year renovation | Will need to rebuild entertainment (pre-closure: 4 nights/week) |

**Pattern:** When properties go dark, entertainment returns when:
1. **New leadership arrives** with a vision (Waldorf, Langham)
2. **A rebrand or renovation creates opportunity** (San Ysidro, Rosewood Hotel Georgia)
3. **Market conditions allow it** (Tonga Room post-COVID)
4. **Competitive pressure demands it** (Four Seasons Biltmore reopening into a market where competitors all program entertainment)

**Oscar Gonzalez fits pattern #1 and #4.** He is new leadership at a property with zero entertainment, in a market where 7 of 8 competitors program live music. Every property in his career history programmed entertainment. The Lodge is the anomaly.

### Question 3: Any Data on What Happens to Guest Satisfaction When Entertainment Is Cut?

**Direct entertainment-specific data is scarce. Broader amenity-cut data is damning.**

No published study isolates the guest satisfaction impact of cutting live entertainment specifically. However:

- **87% of consumers** will spend less or stop spending with brands that cut services (UJET)
- **48%** share bad experiences; **33%** switch to competitors
- Hotel satisfaction dropped **5%** across the industry after COVID service cuts (ACSI)
- The OpenTable anomaly at the Lodge — ranked #1 for "live music" with zero music — proves **guests expect entertainment at this property** and are searching for it

**Relevant positive data from Batch 7:**
- **82%** say experiences are more enjoyable with live music (BMI/NRG)
- **80%** stay longer; **60%** buy more food and drinks
- **5-10%** check average increase; **~25%** revenue jump on live music nights
- **£107K** average annual sales increase per UK venue with live music

**The logic:** If adding live music produces 5-10% check increases and 25% revenue jumps, then removing it produces the inverse. The Lodge went from 4 nights/week of entertainment to zero — and OpenTable guests are still searching for it.

## Three Questions

1. **Hardest implementation decision in this session?** Whether to frame February–March as "just in time" or "slightly late" for the Oscar pitch. The formal budget cycle says it's late (budgets were set in Q4 2025). But the operational reality says it's just in time — entertainment at the Lodge wasn't budgeted at all, so there's nothing to miss. Oscar has to make a discretionary F&B decision regardless of timing. Chose "just in time" because it's more accurate and more useful for PFE's urgency framing. The alternative — "you're late, the budget cycle passed" — would discourage action when in fact the opposite is true.

2. **What did you consider changing but left alone, and why?** Considered downplaying the UJET consumer survey data (87% will spend less with cost-cutting brands) because it covers all industries, not luxury hotels specifically. Left it in because: (a) no luxury-hotel-specific equivalent exists; (b) luxury hotel guests are arguably MORE sensitive to service cuts than average consumers (they're paying premium prices); (c) the directional insight — guests punish brands that cut amenities — is universally applicable. Also considered adding the Ryman Hospitality Properties earnings data (Gaylord Hotels, Grand Ole Opry) as entertainment-driven hotel programming evidence, but it's a fundamentally different model (convention hotels with entertainment venues) and would weaken the luxury resort comparison.

3. **Least confident about going into the final assembly?** Whether the seasonal planning data is specific enough. No source says "luxury F&B Directors make summer entertainment decisions in February-March." The evidence is assembled from general hotel budget cycles (Aug-Dec), entertainment agency observations (September budget meetings), competitor behavior (Grand Del Mar dates already published), and industry timelines (festivals lock 6-9 months out). The inference is strong but it's an inference, not a documented fact. The final report should present this as a logical framework, not an industry standard.
